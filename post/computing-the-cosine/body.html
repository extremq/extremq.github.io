Ah, the ancient field of trigonometry. Who would've thought that, after all these centuries, the sine and the cosine functions would be this important?
Some people probably thought that, but that doesn't matter now.

What matters now is how we compute them. Since they are so important, people should have figured out smart ways of computing these functions. Of course, we will figure some by ourselves, too.

Reader, arm up. We're going on a math trip. These are our beloved functions, graphed on a cartesian plane.

You probably heard about the fact that these functions are periodic. What does that mean?
Well, it means that these functions repeat, and they do so at intervals of \(2\pi\) units.
Knowing this, we can formulate the first important property:

Property 1: periodicity

\(\sin(x) = \sin(2\pi\cdot\lfloor\frac{x}{2\pi}\rfloor + x\bmod 2\pi) = \sin(x\bmod 2\pi)\)

Here, \(\lfloor x\rfloor\) is the and it means taking only the integer part of \(x.\)
For example, \(\lfloor 2.73\rfloor = 2\).

Then, the . \(x \bmod y\) retrieves the remainder after calculating the division of \(\frac{x}{y}\).
For example, \(10.5\bmod 3 = 1.5\) since \(10.5 = 3 * 3 + 1.5\).

All in all, what we have here means that for any \(x\), it is suffice to compute \(\sin(x\bmod 2\pi)\) if we want to know the value of \(\sin(x)\).
This is the most important property, since it allows us to compute the sine value of huge numbers the same way we do for tiny ones.
